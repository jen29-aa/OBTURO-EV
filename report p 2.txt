INTRODUCTION
In today’s fast-paced world, travelers seek seamless and efficient airport experiences. NFC (Near
Field Communication) technology is emerging as a game-changer in the aviation industry, rev￾olutionizing the way passengers navigate airports and enhancing their overall travel experience.
NFC-enabled airport apps are transforming traditional airport processes, offering a plethora of
benefits to both passengers and airport operators.
An NFC-enabled airport app is a mobile application that utilizes NFC technology to facilitate con￾tactless interactions between passengers and NFC-enabled infrastructure within an airport. These
interactions enable a range of functionalities, streamlining various aspects of the passenger jour￾ney, from check-in to boarding.
NFC technology relies on radio waves to transmit data between two devices when they are brought
close together. NFC-enabled airport apps utilize this technology by embedding NFC chips or
tags within airport infrastructure, such as kiosks, scanners, and beacons. When a passenger’s
NFC-enabled smartphone or passport approaches these NFC-enabled elements, data is exchanged
wirelessly, enabling seamless interactions and providing valuable information.
1.1 Problem Statement
Traditional airport processes are often cumbersome, time-consuming, and inefficient, leading to
frustration and inconvenience for passengers. The current system relies heavily on paper docu￾mentation, manual data entry, and physical interactions, creating bottlenecks and delays at various
stages of the passenger journey. Additionally, the current system lacks personalization, making it
difficult for passengers to access relevant information and tailored recommendations.

1. Reduce wait times and congestion at check-in counters, baggage drop stations, and boarding

2.1 ANDA:An Innovative Micro-Location Mobile Ticketing Solu￾tion Based on NFC and BLE Technologies.
Mobile ticketing services allow urban transport passengers to travel in a convenient and easy way,
enhancing their travelling experience. In recent years several mobile ticketing services have started
to be developed and launched, but there is still a lot to be done in terms of its effectiveness,
efficiency and innovation. This paper presents a micro-location mobile ticketing solution based on
Near Field Communication (NFC) and Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) technologies, called Anda.
This solution is based on a check-in/be-out scheme and requires the minimum intervention from
the passenger. It is really innovative in the urban transport field, as it takes advantage of BLE
technology not usually used for this purpose, it is based on a concept of post-billing with a fare
optimization algorithm associated and it allows the micro-location of passengers throughout their
journeys. This paper details the architecture of the solution and its mode of operation. It also
presents the evaluation methodology that was followed during the pilot trial that took place in the
Metropolitan Area of Porto (AMP), Portugal, during one year with 140 real passengers. A set of
design lessons were identified as a result of the field tests and materialized in five mobile ticketing
design dimensions, constituting important contributions to the design of future mobile ticketing
services. Anda was commercially deployed in the AMP in 2018 and is used by thousands of
passengers every day.
2.1.1 Methodology of ANDA Mobile Tracking Solution
The evaluation of the proposed mobile ticketing solution consisted on testing the mobile applica￾tion and the other components during a pilot trial. It was tested in a real environment, in the city
of Porto. The tests involved 140 real passengers, who used the mobile application during their
daily trips. In the first months the participants could only use the Anda in four pre-selected lines

2.2 SCsec: A Secure Near Field Communication System via Screen
Camera Communication
With the increasing popularity of short range communication services, such as contact less pay￾ment and electronic toll collection, screen camera communication and near field communication
(NFC) have received considerable attention from both industry and academia. Existing short range
communication technologies face challenges from deployment, compatibility, and security issues.
In this work, we target the security challenges, specifically the eavesdropping attack, of screen
camera communication systems. We propose SCsec, a secure screen-camera communication sys￾tem. On one hand, SCsec achieves computationally secure communication between the transmitter
and receiver, preventing the eavesdroppers beyond a certain range from learning anything about
the content of the communication. On the other hand, SCsec achieves throughput that is compara￾ble to existing screen camera communication schemes. We extensively evaluate the performance
of our system with real world experiments on multiple devices. Numerous experiment results
show that our system achieves both high security and comparable communication efficiency with
existing screen camera communication systems.
2.2.1 Proposed Model
The SCsec system consists of two main components: the transmitter and the receiver.
Transmitter: The transmitter is responsible for generating the data to be transmitted, encrypt￾ing the data, and modulating the encrypted data onto the screen. The data is encrypted using a
symmetric-key encryption algorithm. The encrypted data is then modulated onto the screen using
a light modulation technique. The modulated data is then captured by the receiver.
Receiver : The receiver is responsible for capturing the modulated data, demodulating the data,
and decrypting the decrypted data. The modulated data is captured by the camera. The demodu￾lated data is then decrypted using the same symmetric-key encryption algorithm that was used by
the transmitter. The decrypted data is then processed by the receiver.
SCsec system utilizes several security mechanisms to achieve secure communication:
Encryption: Encryption is used to protect the confidentiality of the transmitted data. Only the
intended receiver can decrypt the data.
Physical layer security: Physical layer security is used to protect the integrity of the transmitted
data. Eavesdroppers who are not in close proximity to the transmitter and receiver will not be able
to capture the transmitted data.

Authentication: Authentication is used to ensure that the receiver is authentic. This prevents
man-in-the-middle attacks.
The goal is to securely transmit data from the transmitter to the legal receiver with data rate
comparable to current screen camera communication systems. In this work, we assume that the
eavesdropping device of the attacker is offthe-shelf camera with similar capability of mobile de￾vices. Further, we do not consider jamming attack in this paper. The jamming signal of screen
camera communication, which is also visible light, can be easily detected and located by Alice
and Bob. To achieve this goal, we decompose the problem into two subproblems: key distribution
and data communication.
2.2.2 Experimental Setup
The boundary for legal receiver and eavesdropper is chosen based on the change of RGB norm

POS Payment Application (PPA), Elliptic Curve Digital Signature Algorithm (ECDSA) is used
